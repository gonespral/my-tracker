What is the clock?

The small triangle on your calorie ring is a progress ticker. It shows how many calories you would typically have eaten by this point in the day, based on your 30-day meal history. The position is always driven by the current time — not by which meals you have logged today.

If the ring's filled arc is ahead of the ticker, you are eating faster than your typical pace. If the filled arc is behind, you are pacing slower than usual and have room to spare.

How the position is calculated

1
Builds a per-meal average from the last 30 days

For each meal type (breakfast, lunch, snack, dinner) it sums the calories logged and divides by the number of days that meal was actually recorded. Days where a meal was skipped do not drag the average down.

2
Maps each meal to a time window

The eating day runs from 7 am to 10 pm. Each meal is expected to be complete by a fixed point: breakfast by 9 am, lunch by 1 pm, snack by 4 pm, dinner by 8 pm.

3
Interpolates expected calories for the current time

Past a meal's deadline its full average is included. Within a meal's window a proportional fraction is counted. The result is the total calories you would typically have consumed by now.

4
Divides by today's calorie target

Expected calories ÷ target gives the ring fraction — the exact position of the ticker on the ring.

Ticker states

Hidden
Before 7 am

The eating window has not started. Ticker stays hidden.

Grey — behind pace
You have eaten less than your historical average for this time

The ring's filled arc is behind the ticker. You're eating slower than usual — plenty of room left in the day.

Orange — ahead of pace
You have eaten more than your historical average for this time

The ring's filled arc has passed the ticker. You're eating faster than usual for this hour. The ticker turns orange as a heads-up.

Red — pace exceeds target
Your historical eating pace has exceeded today's target

The ticker snaps to 12 o'clock and turns red. This means if you ate at your typical rate, you'd already be over target by now — usually happens late evening or on low-target days.

No history yet?

If you have no 30-day meal data, the ticker falls back to a plain time-of-day position — it advances linearly from 0% at 7 am to 100% at 10 pm. This gives a rough orientation until your history builds up.

Meals you have never logged

If a particular meal type has no history at all, MyTracker assigns it the average of your other known meals as a stand-in so the calculation always has something sensible to work with.

The ticker is a pace guide, not a strict target. The ring always shows your real logged calories accurately. The ticker reflects typical-you at this hour, not a rule about what you must eat.
