Calorie counting

How MyTracker tracks what you eat, what you burn, and what that means for your goals.

The basic idea

Your body needs a certain number of calories each day just to function — to breathe, pump blood, regulate temperature, and everything else that happens at rest. On top of that, any movement you do burns additional calories. Calorie counting is the practice of measuring how much you eat versus how much you burn, so you can steer your weight intentionally over time.

MyTracker does the arithmetic for you. You log what you eat and what you do; it shows you where you stand against your target and how that accumulates week over week.

The calorie ring on the Today tab fills as you log food. Its target is your base goal plus any eat-back from exercise. When the ring is full you have hit your target; going past it means you have eaten more than planned.

Macronutrients

Food calories come from three macronutrients. Each gram contributes a different amount of energy:

Protein
4 kcal / gram
Carbohydrate
4 kcal / gram
Fat
9 kcal / gram

The three small rings below the calorie ring on the Today tab track each macro against your daily target. Claude estimates all three when you log a meal in plain language or from a photo.

How your calorie target is set

1
Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Your BMR is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest. MyTracker calculates it from your age, sex, height, and weight using the Mifflin–St Jeor formula. Enter these in Settings → Calories.

2
Activity level multiplier

Your BMR is scaled by an activity multiplier (1.2 for sedentary up to 1.9 for very active) to give your Total Daily Energy Expenditure — how many calories you actually need on an average day.

3
Goal adjustment

If you want to lose weight, a calorie deficit is subtracted from your TDEE. The result is your base goal — the same every day before exercise is factored in.

4
Exercise eat-back

On days you log a workout, your target increases automatically. By default 50% of the net active calories burned are added back — this is the eat-back. Net active means gross burn minus the resting calories your BMR already accounts for. You can change the percentage or disable eat-back entirely in Settings → Calories.

Deficits, surpluses, and weight change

A sustained calorie deficit causes your body to draw on stored energy (mostly fat) and you lose weight over time. A surplus means you are taking in more than you burn and weight trends up. One kilogram of body fat contains roughly 7,700 kcal, so a daily deficit of 500 kcal leads to about 0.5 kg of loss per week.

In practice the relationship is not perfectly linear — water retention, muscle gain, and day-to-day variation all cause the scale to fluctuate. MyTracker's weight chart shows the trend over weeks, which is more informative than any single weigh-in.

Calorie estimates — whether from food databases, Claude's AI estimates, or smartwatch sensors — carry real uncertainty. Use the numbers as a consistent reference over time rather than treating them as exact measurements.
